Chapter 1 – Basic Concepts of Mobile Networks

This chapter introduces the fundamentals of mobile networks, explaining key concepts, the evolution of generations, main components, and how everything works so your phone can connect to the internet and make calls. It is designed to be easy to understand even without technical background.

1.1 What are mobile networks?

Mobile networks are wireless communication systems that allow devices to connect without cables, enabling communication and internet access while moving, at home, in a car, or anywhere. The main feature is mobility — your device does not need to be physically connected.

These networks use radio waves to transmit information between your device, known as User Equipment (UE), and radio towers deployed across the territory. These towers connect to a core system called the core network, which manages communication, authentication, and data routing to the internet.

Why doesn’t your phone lose signal when you move?

When you move, your device automatically switches between antennas — a process called handover. This happens seamlessly so calls, videos, and browsing continue without interruption.

1.2 Evolution of mobile generations: from 2G to 5G

Mobile technology has evolved significantly through generations, commonly called G (generation). Each one brought improvements in speed, capacity, and architecture.

GenerationMain FeaturesTypical SpeedExamples
2G Digital communication and SMS, voice-focused. Up to 64 kbps GSM, CDMA
3G Mobile internet and video calls. Up to several Mbps UMTS, HSPA
4G All-IP network, high speed, low latency. Tens to hundreds of Mbps LTE
5G Ultra-fast speed, near-zero latency, massive IoT support. Gbps range NR (New Radio)

How do these generations impact daily life?

  • 2G: Enabled text messaging and digital voice calls.
  • 3G: Made mobile internet usable for browsing and basic video.
  • 4G: Enabled HD streaming, online gaming, and smooth video calls.
  • 5G: Enables smart cities, autonomous cars, and massive IoT connectivity.

1.3 Main components of a mobile network

1.3.1 UE (User Equipment)

Your phone, tablet, or connected device. Each UE has a SIM card containing its identity (IMSI). It is the starting point of communication.

1.3.2 Radio Access Network (RAN)

The part of the network responsible for wireless communication.

  • 4G: eNodeB (radio base station)
  • 5G: gNodeB

1.3.3 Core Network

The “brain” of the network, responsible for mobility, authentication, routing, and connectivity.

  • MME: mobility and authentication control
  • HSS: subscriber database
  • SGW: data routing
  • PGW: internet gateway

1.4 Bearers and Quality of Service (QoS)

Data is carried through logical channels called bearers, each with different priority levels.

Voice requires low latency, while file downloads tolerate delays.

QoS ensures each type of traffic receives proper treatment.

Types of bearers

  • Default bearer: created automatically for general data
  • Dedicated bearer: reserved for specific services like voice/video

1.7 Mobility and handover

When moving during a call or data session, the device switches towers without interruption. This is called handover.

  • Intra-eNodeB: within same tower
  • Inter-eNodeB: between towers
  • Inter-RAT: between technologies (e.g., 4G to 3G)

MME coordinates these transitions to avoid service interruption.

1.8 Mobile network security

  • Authentication: verifies user identity via SIM
  • Encryption: protects data from interception
  • Integrity: ensures data is not modified

1.12 The hidden side: security

Mobile networks were not originally designed for modern cyberattacks, which creates vulnerabilities that evolved over time.

1.13 Security evolution

GenerationSecurity Model
2G/3GFull trust (SS7)
4GTrusted partners (Diameter)
5GAPI-based exposure

Security improved, but risks remain — they just changed form.
