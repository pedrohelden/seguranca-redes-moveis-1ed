Chapter 13 – Business Impact

Up to this point, we have seen how attacks happen, how they work, and how they can be detected and mitigated.

But there is one question every executive asks:

👉 “What is the real business impact of this?”

This chapter connects technical security with financial, regulatory, and reputational risk.

13.1 Why telecom security is critical

Telecom operators are not just connectivity providers — they are part of a country’s critical infrastructure.

  • They support banking systems (SMS OTP authentication)
  • They enable digital identity verification
  • They carry sensitive data

👉 An attack does not only impact the operator — it impacts the entire ecosystem.

13.2 Direct financial impact

💸 Telecom fraud

Fraud typeImpact
Call fraudUnnecessary operational costs
Roaming abuseInterconnection losses
Charging manipulationRevenue loss

Example:

An attacker can generate thousands of international calls by manipulating signaling.

👉 Result: direct and immediate financial loss.

💰 OTP interception (indirect impact)

SMS interception does not directly cost the operator money — but it causes something worse:

  • Banking fraud
  • Account takeovers
  • User trust degradation

👉 The operator becomes part of the problem.

13.3 Reputational impact

Trust is a telecom operator’s most important asset.

When security flaws are exploited:

  • Customers lose confidence
  • The brand is associated with insecurity
  • Competitors gain advantage

👉 Problem:

Reputation is easy to lose and hard to rebuild.

13.4 Regulatory impact (LGPD and compliance)

In Brazil, the LGPD (General Data Protection Law) imposes clear obligations:

  • Protection of personal data
  • Prevention of data leaks
  • Incident response requirements
RiskConsequence
Location leakagePrivacy violation
IMSI exposureCompromised sensitive data
Security failureFines and sanctions

👉 Penalties can reach millions.

13.5 Operational impact

Attacks also directly affect network operations:

  • Service outages
  • Saturation of network elements (HSS, MME, etc.)
  • Performance degradation

Example: Diameter Storm

Failure → retransmissions → overload → collapse

Result:

  • Network unavailability
  • Failed calls
  • Unstable internet

👉 Direct impact on customer experience.

13.6 Cost of mitigation vs cost of incident

Aspect Prevention Incident
Cost Controlled Unpredictable
Impact Limited High
Reputation Protected Damaged

👉 Conclusion:

Prevention is far cheaper than remediation.

13.7 Security as a competitive advantage

More mature operators use security as a differentiator:

  • They offer fraud protection
  • They ensure user privacy
  • They meet regulatory requirements

👉 Security becomes value instead of cost.

13.8 Metrics that matter to business

Metric Meaning
Fraud loss Direct financial impact
Detection time Speed of response
Mitigation time Operational efficiency
Number of incidents Exposure level

👉 Security must be measurable.

13.9 Executive translation

For leadership, everything comes down to three questions:

  • What is the financial risk?
  • What is the reputational impact?
  • Are we protected?

👉 Role of the technical team:

Translate technical threats into business impact.

13.10 Conclusion

Mobile network security is not just a technical problem.

  • It is a business problem
  • It is a regulatory problem
  • It is a trust problem

👉 And in the end, everything converges to one thing:

Money.

Those who invest in security protect revenue, customers, and the future.
